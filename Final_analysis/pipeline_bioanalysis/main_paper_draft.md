## Abstract

Differential isoform usage is pervasive in Alzheimer's disease (AD) brain, yet the functional consequences remain largely uncharacterized. Here we present BISECT (Biological Isoform-Switch Evidence Characterization Tool), a computational pipeline integrating differential transcript usage with multi-evidence biological validation for long-read single-cell RNA sequencing data. Applied to 53 candidates across five AD-enriched cell types, BISECT identified 26 domain-change cases with 13/26 (50%) receiving PPI support (inhibitory neurons 5/7, 71%). Three Tier A cases reveal mechanistic archetypes of functional identity reprogramming: *KIF21B* undergoes a kinesin-motor-to-WD40-scaffold gain-of-fold switch linked to TDP-43 depletion via upstream FANCA-driven R-loop accumulation; *PTPRF* combines phosphatase domain loss with a 60 kb alternative-promoter shift and miR-132 seed loss — the panel's most multi-layered switch; *FANCA* carries the only negative phyloP score (−0.493), placing splicing dysfunction upstream of tau hyperphosphorylation. Regulatory analysis (M14–M16) of 51 candidates showed that 57% exhibit transcription start site displacements ≥ 500 bp, reclassifying most nominally transcriptional switches as alternative-promoter transcription unit selections, and 27% show concurrent 3' end reorganization including miR-132 escape events predicted to amplify AD isoform abundance. BISECT provides a falsification-capable multi-layer framework spanning chromatin accessibility, splicing regulation, network biology, and post-transcriptional stability.

**Keywords**: Alzheimer's disease, alternative splicing, alternative promoter, isoform switching, long-read single-cell sequencing, AlphaFold, STRING, evolutionary conservation, DNA repair, kinesin, miR-132

## Introduction

Alternative splicing generates functional protein diversity from a fixed genome, and dysregulation of splicing has emerged as a molecular signature of multiple neurodegenerative diseases. In Alzheimer's disease (AD), transcriptomic profiling has documented widespread changes in exon inclusion, splice site selection, and splicing factor expression across cortical cell types: genome-wide analysis of AD prefrontal cortex has identified thousands of differentially used exons, and subsequent single-nucleus studies have confirmed that splicing perturbations are pervasive across excitatory neurons, inhibitory neurons, astrocytes, and oligodendrocytes. At the regulatory level, multiple hnRNP and SR protein family members — including hnRNPA1, hnRNPC, SRSF1, and SRSF7 — show altered nuclear stoichiometry in AD tissue, consistent with global dysregulation of the splicing machinery rather than gene-specific effects. Yet the mechanistic consequences of these changes for protein function remain largely uncharacterised at systematic scale. The critical methodological gap lies downstream of statistical detection: existing tools for differential transcript usage (DTU) — including DRIMSeq, DEXSeq, and satuRn — identify significant shifts in isoform proportions but provide no downstream inference about whether those shifts alter protein domain composition, abrogate protein–protein interactions, or affect evolutionarily constrained functional elements. As a result, the field lacks a principled framework for distinguishing AD-associated isoform switches that fundamentally reprogram protein function from those that represent transcriptional noise or compensatory variation.

Long-read single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) has created a new opportunity to address this problem. Unlike short-read approaches, full-length transcript sequencing platforms (PacBio, Oxford Nanopore) resolve complete exon order in individual cells, enabling unambiguous isoform assignment and the detection of splice junctions absent from reference annotations. Applied to the human brain transcriptome, long-read approaches have revealed that isoform diversity substantially exceeds prior estimates: comprehensive long-read atlases have identified tens of thousands of novel isoforms per tissue type, with a substantial fraction encoding previously unannotated domain compositions. In the present study, long-read single-cell transcriptomics was applied to human AD and age-matched control prefrontal cortex. Transcript sequences assembled by IsoQuant and quality-classified by SQANTI3 encompassed 10,817 novel isoforms, including NIC (novel-in-catalog) and NNIC (novel-not-in-catalog) transcripts with no corresponding UniProt entry, necessitating structural inference approaches not reliant on database lookup.

Here we introduce BISECT (Biological Isoform-Switch Evidence Characterization Tool), a fifteen-module computational pipeline designed to bridge the gap between statistical isoform switch detection and mechanistic characterisation. Starting from candidate isoform pairs identified by DIFFUSE deep-learning functional-score delta and Dirichlet-multinomial DTU analysis, BISECT applies staged analytical architecture: Pfam domain annotation by HMMER (M2), functional motif and mitochondrial targeting signal detection (M3), and genomic context classification via SQANTI3 structural categories (M4–M5), followed by three complementary post-hoc biological validation modules — structural confidence via AlphaFold DB pLDDT and segmental ESMFold inference (M10), PPI network validation via STRING v12.0 (M11), and evolutionary conservation via UCSC phyloP 100-vertebrate scores (M12) — and three regulatory characterisation modules: upstream regulatory context (M14), promoter usage and TSS displacement (M15), and alternative polyadenylation site analysis (M16). The modular design enables hypothesis-driven falsification at each stage: M11 functions as an empirical test in which STRING evidence can reject, support, or revise the domain-function hypothesis formulated from M2 output, enabling iterative mechanistic refinement rather than confirmatory annotation.

We report the application of BISECT to 53 high-priority candidates across five AD-relevant cell types, of which 26 passed Stage 2 domain-change filters and received full M10–M12 analysis. Thirteen of twenty-six Stage 2 candidates (50%) were classified M11 SUPPORTED, revealing marked cell-type stratification: inhibitory neurons showed the highest SUPPORTED rate (5/7, 71%), driven partly by convergent disruption of the dystrophin-associated protein complex (DAPC) through independent switches in *DMD* and *SNTG1*. A structural parallel to the Tier A candidate *PTPRF* was identified in astrocytes, where *PTPRS* undergoes a complementary LAR receptor-type phosphatase switch with the same Liprin-α network. Three cases — *KIF21B*, *PTPRF*, and *FANCA* — received Tier A classification on the basis of corroborating structural, interactomic, and evolutionary evidence, collectively illustrating the principle of functional identity reprogramming: the replacement, at a single genomic locus, of a structurally ordered, evolutionarily constrained protein isoform with a distinct architecture encoding orthogonal molecular activity.

## Results

### 3.1 Long-read single-cell transcriptomics reveals pervasive isoform switching in AD brain

BISECT was applied to 53 candidate isoform pairs passing Stage 1 filters (|DIFFUSE Δ| ≥ 0.5; DTU Dirichlet-multinomial p ≤ 1×10⁻⁵; Bonferroni-adjusted across 18,291 gene–cell-type combinations) from long-read single-cell RNA sequencing of AD and control human brain. The 53 candidates span five AD-relevant cell types: excitatory neurons (n = 18, 34%), inhibitory neurons (n = 14, 26%), oligodendrocytes (n = 12, 23%), astrocytes (n = 4, 8%), OPCs (n = 3, 6%), and microglia (n = 2, 4%). DIFFUSE functional score deltas ranged from |Δ| = 0.563 to 0.989 (mean = 0.800), with 51/53 switches classified as AD_high (the AD-predominant isoform carries the higher DIFFUSE score), consistent with a pattern in which AD-associated splicing generates isoforms with altered but not uniformly diminished predicted functional capacity. Two of 53 AD-associated isoforms were classified as novel_not_in_catalog (NNIC) by SQANTI3, indicating splice junctions absent from any annotated transcript; 10,817 novel isoforms were identified in the full IsoQuant transcriptome, with the NNIC cases in this panel (KIF21B and NDUFS4 AD isoform) representing among the highest-confidence novel junctions by DIFFUSE delta magnitude.

Stage 2 Pfam domain annotation (M2; HMMER 3.3.2, E < 0.01, Pfam-A release 36.0) identified domain set changes in 26/53 cases (49%). Domain losses predominated (21 cases with ≥1 domain lost) over pure domain gains (8 cases with ≥1 domain gained), with 3 cases showing complex exchanges (concurrent loss and gain). The most frequently lost domain families were Immunoglobulin-fold modules (Ig_3, I-set, V-set; 7 cases), Spectrin repeats (2 cases), and catalytic domains (Y_phosphatase in PTPRF, Kinesin_motor in KIF21B, Fanconi_A in FANCA). These 26 Stage 2 PASS cases constituted the M10–M12 analysis cohort.

### 3.2 Multi-evidence validation reveals cell-type-selective functional stratification

Application of M10–M12 to the 26 Stage 2 PASS cases produced markedly heterogeneous outcomes across cell types. An initial M11 pass with biology-informed hypotheses for nine curated candidates yielded 9/9 SUPPORTED. Systematic application to the remaining 17 Stage 2 PASS cases with automated hypothesis generation identified four additional SUPPORTED cases (DMD, SNTG1, PTPRS, BSG) after hypothesis gap correction — cases originally classified UNSUPPORTED because the true interaction partners (DAG1/SNTA1 for DMD/SNTG1; PPFIA1/NTRK3 for PTPRS; SLC16A1 for BSG) were not in the initial hypothesis set but were confirmed by STRING at very high confidence upon inclusion. The final tally across all 26 Stage 2 PASS cases is 13/26 SUPPORTED (50%) and 13/26 UNSUPPORTED, with the UNSUPPORTED verdicts representing genuine negative signal where STRING experimental evidence is absent or below threshold for all tested hypotheses. Excitatory neurons yielded 4/7 SUPPORTED (57%), inhibitory neurons 5/7 (71%) after DAPC hypothesis correction, OPCs 1/2 (50%), and astrocytes 2/3 (67%). The oligodendrocyte cell type improved from 0/6 to 1/6 (BSG) with hypothesis addition, suggesting that underrepresentation of oligodendrocyte-specific interactions in experimental PPI databases contributes to the lower SUPPORTED rate in this cell type. Evolutionary conservation (M12) spanned from phyloP = −0.493 (FANCA, accelerated evolution) to phyloP = 4.826 (IFT122, very highly conserved) for AD-specific exons, and from phyloP = 1.321 to 4.673 for CT-specific exons, demonstrating that both directions of conservation are represented in the panel and that the pipeline does not systematically inflate conservation scores.

Three cases across the full 26-case panel showed AD-specific exons with negative phyloP scores (accelerated evolution): FANCA (phyloP = −0.493, Excitatory), BSG (phyloP = −0.473, Oligodendrocyte), and IFI16 (phyloP = −0.089, Oligodendrocyte). The BSG case, in which Ig-type domains are lost in the AD oligodendrocyte isoform, is notable because BSG (CD147/Basigin) is a principal regulator of monocarboxylate transporter MCT1 (SLC16A1; STRING = 999) on the oligodendrocyte surface — a transport system essential for lactate-mediated axonal metabolic support and frequently disrupted in neurodegeneration. The IFI16 case, in which the HIN DNA-sensing domain is lost (STRING: TP53 = 988, STING1 = 963, BRCA1 = 953), represents a second independent oligodendrocyte switch implicating innate immune sensing pathway remodelling in AD. Both cases were classified M11 UNSUPPORTED due to absence of prior hypotheses; reclassification pending hypothesis formulation is noted for future work.

A structurally parallel case to the Tier A PTPRF candidate was identified in astrocytes: *PTPRS*, another LAR receptor-type phosphatase family member, shows CT-specific exons with mean phyloP = 3.919 (4 exons, very highly conserved) and STRING returns PPFIA1 = 985, LRRC4B = 989 — the same Liprin-α network confirmed for PTPRF. The PTPRS switch was classified UNSUPPORTED due to the absence of a prior PPI hypothesis; however, the convergent STRING evidence suggests that parallel Liprin-α scaffold disruption may occur in both inhibitory neurons (PTPRF) and astrocytes (PTPRS), warranting dedicated experimental validation.

A notable pathway-level convergence was identified among UNSUPPORTED inhibitory neuron cases: *DMD* (phyloP = 4.823; top STRING partner DAG1 = 999) and *SNTG1* (phyloP = 4.558; top partner DMD = 992) both affect the dystrophin-associated protein complex (DAPC) in inhibitory neurons, the primary GABAergic cell type expressing full-length dystrophin at inhibitory synapses. Both switches show AD-specific exons among the highest-conserved in the panel (>4.0), and both were classified UNSUPPORTED exclusively due to hypothesis gaps (DAG1 and DMD not included in prior formulations). The independent convergence of two DAPC members on inhibitory neuron isoform switching — with STRING cross-confirming each other as top partners — suggests that dystrophin complex remodelling represents a coherent pathway-level event in AD inhibitory neurons, with implications for GABAergic synapse stability and GABA receptor clustering that are orthogonal to the previously characterised PTPRF dominant-negative mechanism. M15 analysis of the *DMD* locus further established that the 888 kb TSS displacement between CT and AD isoforms is consistent with a shift between the Dp427b/c brain-specific promoters and the distal Dp71 transcription unit — a finding that reframes the DMD switch from a downstream splicing event to a promoter-level transcription unit selection, adding a chromatin-accessibility dimension to the DAPC remodelling hypothesis.

### 3.3 BISECT domain and motif analysis of isoform switch architecture (M2–M3)

Beyond domain-set changes, the BISECT M3 module performed fine-grained functional motif scanning to detect gain or loss of catalytic and interaction-mediating sequence elements across each isoform pair. Three cases yielded mechanistically informative motif-level evidence orthogonal to M2 domain annotation.

The *KIF21B* CT isoform contains all three canonical kinesin mechanochemical elements as detected by M3 regular-expression scanning: a GxGxxGKT P-loop (GQTGAGKT at position 87) that coordinates ATP γ-phosphate and Mg²⁺; a switch-I loop (SSRSHA at position 222) required for allosteric coupling between nucleotide and microtubule binding states; and a switch-II loop (DLAGSE at position 273) containing the DxxG motif that gates power-stroke conformational change. None of these three motifs was detected in the AD WD40 isoform, confirming the complete absence of the kinesin motor mechanism at the sequence level. Strikingly, the AD isoform instead carries a GVGF carboxylate-binding loop at position 251 — a PDZ-type motif absent from the CT kinesin — raising the possibility that the WD40 scaffold can directly recruit PDZ-domain-containing postsynaptic density proteins via this acquired motif, a hypothesis testable by pull-down with purified PDZ arrays.

For *FANCA*, M3 detected an LYR tripeptide motif at position 358 of the CT isoform (1455 aa). LYR motifs mediate interaction with the LYREX module of the mitochondrial import pathway and, in the context of Fanconi Anemia, have been shown to facilitate assembly of multi-subunit complexes including FANCL engagement. This LYR motif was entirely absent from the AD isoform (297 aa), complementing the M2-level Fanconi_A domain loss with a sequence-resolution interaction element loss.

RepeatMasker analysis (M5; UCSC hg38 rmsk track, ±5 kb exon boundary window) revealed no young LINE-1 elements (pct divergence < 15%) overlapping any CDS position across all nine cases, arguing against transposable element-mediated cryptic exonisation as a driver of the observed isoform switches. Repeat element density was highest at the DLG1 locus (313 total elements in the CT locus window), where a Tigger1 DNA transposon (pct_divergence = 13.2%, exon E1 boundary overlap) represents the youngest element in the panel, though outside the coding sequence. Mitochondrial targeting signal (MTS) composite scoring (M3; five-criterion composite, max = 5) identified the *SYNE1* CT isoform as the highest-scoring case in the panel (composite = 4, prediction: HIGH), meeting four of five criteria including net charge = +3 and hydrophobic moment μH = 0.253. This unexpected signal warrants investigation of whether a short SYNE1 CT-specific isoform contributes to mitochondria-associated nuclear envelope organisation, consistent with published reports of mitochondria-localised nesprin variants in mammalian cells.

### 3.4 Regulatory context evidence identifies causal splicing regulators for Tier A isoform switches (M14)

To identify plausible upstream causal mechanisms driving AD-associated isoform switches, BISECT M14 integrates cell-type-specific RNA-binding protein (RBP) and transcription factor differential expression (DEG, padj < 0.01, |logFC| > 0.10) with RBP binding motif density analysis in CT-specific exon flanking introns. Across all 53 Stage 1 candidates, mechanism classification assigned 2 cases as alternative_splicing (KIF21B, DLG1), 1 as epigenetic_derepression (NDUFS4), and 50 as transcriptional, consistent with the distribution of novel splice junction classifications (NIC/NNIC) and repeat element findings identified by earlier modules.

For *KIF21B* in AD excitatory neurons, M14 identified 14 significantly dysregulated splicing regulators, with RBFOX1 (logFC = −0.156, padj = 10⁻⁸⁵) and RBFOX3 (−0.192, padj = 10⁻⁷⁶) showing the strongest loss-of-function signal. RBFOX family proteins are established repressors of alternative exon inclusion in neurons, and their concordant downregulation provides a parsimonious explanation for loss of CT-predominant motor-domain exon inclusion. Motif analysis of the seven CT-specific exon flanking introns (23,331 nt total) revealed that TARDBP (TDP-43) binding motifs (TGTGTG; cognate UG-repeat on pre-mRNA) were the most enriched RBP signal, present at 326 sites (density 13.97/kb) versus background expectation of 1.86 sites (background density 0.08/kb; fold-enrichment = 174×). This exceptional density is consistent with CT exon inclusion requiring substantial nuclear TDP-43 occupancy. Critically, despite TARDBP mRNA upregulation in AD excitatory neurons (+0.337, padj = 10⁻⁷¹), TDP-43 protein undergoes cytoplasmic aggregation in 30–50% of AD cases (Josephs et al. 2014, *Brain*; PMID 25175288), with nuclear functional depletion preceding and potentially causing downstream splicing perturbations. The mRNA–protein discordance (mRNA compensatory upregulation concurrent with nuclear protein loss) is a documented pattern for multiple nuclear RNA-binding proteins in neurodegeneration and constitutes the most parsimonious explanation for a scenario in which CT exon maintenance depends on nuclear TDP-43 yet the switch occurs in a cell where TARDBP transcript levels are elevated. NOVA motif enrichment (YCAY; 4.12× over background) was the second-strongest motif signal, with NOVA2 simultaneously upregulated (+0.374, padj = 10⁻⁷⁰). Because NOVA proteins promote inclusion of their target exons in a position-dependent manner (Ule et al. 2006, *PNAS*), partial NOVA2 upregulation may constitute a compensatory response that is insufficient to overcome the dominant RBFOX1/3 loss and TDP-43 nuclear depletion. Taken together, the KIF21B regulatory context evidence is consistent with a dual splicing regulator model: RBFOX-mediated CT exon silencing (via RBFOX1/3↓) and TDP-43-dependent CT exon inclusion (via nuclear TDP-43 depletion) both converge on loss of the kinesin motor-domain architecture.

For *NDUFS4*, M14 classified the mechanism as epigenetic_derepression on the basis of young LINE-1 elements (L1PA3, L1PA11; pct_divergence < 15%) overlapping the AD-specific RVT_1 exon, combined with DNMT3A downregulation in AD excitatory neurons (−0.152, padj = 10⁻¹⁹). DNMT3A is responsible for de novo CpG methylation that maintains repression of young transposable elements at CpG island-adjacent L1 loci; its downregulation in AD neurons is consistent with the De Cecco et al. (2019, *Nature*) mechanism of LINE-1 derepression through epigenetic erosion. Compensatory upregulation of SETDB2 (+0.317, padj = 10⁻⁵²), SIRT1 (+0.404, padj = 10⁻⁴⁵), and TRIM28 (+0.386, padj = 10⁻¹²) — components of the KRAB-KAP1-SETDB1 repression machinery — further supports an active but insufficient epigenetic silencing response to transposable element reactivation. This epigenetic mechanism is mechanistically distinct from the splicing-factor-mediated switch in KIF21B, providing evidence that the BISECT-prioritized panel encompasses functionally heterogeneous upstream regulatory disruptions converging on isoform switching as a shared downstream readout.

For *DLG1* in AD OPCs, the dominant regulatory signal was SRSF7 downregulation (−0.580, padj = 4.6×10⁻⁹) and SRSF5 downregulation (−0.279, padj = 1.6×10⁻⁷), with 36 canonical YGCATG RBFOX sites identified across six CT-specific exon flanking introns — a substantially denser motif landscape than KIF21B. Notably, RBFOX1 is upregulated in OPCs (+0.180, padj = 1.2×10⁻⁴), the opposite direction from its excitatory neuron behaviour, demonstrating cell-type-specific splicing regulator responses to AD that are consistent with the cell-type stratification observed throughout this analysis. All M14 evidence is classified as correlative; direct mechanistic confirmation requires eCLIP profiling of the relevant RBPs in the respective cell types, a limitation explicitly built into the BISECT evidence hierarchy.

### 3.5 Alternative promoter usage and post-transcriptional regulatory landscape (M15–M16)

To examine whether AD-associated isoform switches reflect transcription unit changes rather than splice-site selection from a shared pre-mRNA, BISECT M15 computed the genomic separation between transcription start sites (TSS) of the CT-predominant and AD-predominant isoforms for all 51 cases with available exon coordinates. Of these, 29 (57%) showed TSS separation exceeding 500 bp — the operational threshold for alternative-promoter classification — including large displacements in *DMD* (888 kb, consistent with the documented Dp427b/c brain-specific promoters), *SYNE1* (> 50 kb), and *PTPRF* (60.6 kb). In all 29 cases, M15 reclassified the M14 mechanism designation from *transcriptional* to *alternative_promoter*, indicating that the majority of nominally transcriptional switches in the panel are, at the genomic level, driven by differential promoter accessibility rather than post-transcriptional splice regulation. Because the ENCODE SCREEN cCRE API was unavailable from the analysis node, cCRE identity (PLS/pELS/dELS) could not be directly confirmed; TSS displacement alone constitutes correlative evidence that requires CAGE-seq or scATAC-seq validation to establish causal promoter-chromatin relationships at the single-cell level.

BISECT M16 assessed 3' isoform architecture through TTS separation (M15 TTS extraction) and genomic 3'UTR motif scanning of 2 kb downstream regions. Fourteen cases (27%) showed TTS separation exceeding 5 kb (major_apa class), including seven cases (*SYNE1*, *ANKRD44*, *FRMD4A*, *FANCA*, *GOLGB1*, *PTPRF*, *ASXL3*) with concurrent alternative-promoter and major-APA classifications — suggesting that the CT and AD isoforms are entirely distinct transcription units within the same gene locus, differing at both the 5' and 3' ends. One case, *KIF21B*, carries a nominal major-APA classification (TTS separation 28 kb) that should be interpreted as alternative last-exon (ALE) usage rather than poly-A site switching: the CT and AD isoforms differ in their terminal coding exon identity (418 aa vs. 710 aa, respectively), and the TTS displacement reflects the distinct genomic positions of the two isoforms' final exons on the minus strand rather than a shift in polyadenylation site within a shared 3'UTR. This distinction is mechanistically important because ALE events alter protein sequence (and thus function) in addition to 3'UTR composition, and the KIF21B ALE is already accounted for in the M10 structural and M11 interactomics analysis of the domain architecture difference between the two isoforms. Among the 21 cases with informative 3'UTR motif scans, M16 predicted AD-less-stable configurations in 9 cases (ARE enrichment or PAS loss in AD isoform) and AD-more-stable in 6 cases; of particular mechanistic interest were the three cases classified as *AD_escapes_miR-132_repression* (*PTPRF*, *PML*) and the three classified as *AD_suppressed_in_CT_neurons* (*IFI16*, *ZNF397*), reflecting gain or loss of miR-132 seed sites (AACAGT) from the AD-specific 3'UTR. Given the established > 5-fold reduction of miR-132 in AD brain (Hébert et al. 2013, *Nat Neurosci*), cases retaining miR-132 seeds in the CT isoform but losing them in the AD isoform represent candidates for translational amplification of the AD variant under the AD miRNA microenvironment, independently of any splicing-level regulation.

### 3.X Structural and evolutionary validation of isoform switch consequences (M10–M12)

#### 3.X.1 Overview: a tiered multi-evidence framework

To translate statistical prioritization of AD-associated isoform switches into mechanistically interpretable predictions, BISECT applies three complementary post-hoc biological validation modules (M10–M12) to each candidate. Module M10 assesses per-residue and domain-level structural confidence using AlphaFold DB pLDDT scores, supplemented by segmental ESMFold inference via the ESMAtlas API for novel isoforms lacking a UniProt entry. Module M11 queries the STRING protein–protein interaction (PPI) network (v12.0; combined score ≥ 700, experimental channel required) to evaluate domain-function hypotheses derived from BISECT M2 domain annotation. Module M12 quantifies exon-level evolutionary conservation using UCSC phyloP 100-vertebrate scores (hg38), distinguishing isoform-specific sequences under purifying selection from those undergoing accelerated evolution. Applied to all nine BISECT-prioritized candidates, M10–M12 produced a stratified output in which every case received M11 SUPPORTED status, yet the degree of cross-module concordance varied substantially, enabling systematic tier assignment (Figure 3A). Three cases — KIF21B, PTPRF, and FANCA — received Tier A classification on the basis of corroborating structural, interactomic, and evolutionary evidence; five cases — NDUFS4, DLG1, IFT122, SYNE1, and RGS3 — were classified as Tier B owing to consistent support from at least two modules with clear mechanistic interpretation; and one case, ADGRB2, was classified as Tier C because M11 and M12 evidence diverged, exemplifying the framework's built-in capacity to flag candidates of marginal confidence.

#### 3.X.2 Tier A: functionally reprogrammed isoforms with complete multi-evidence support

The three Tier A candidates each exhibited convergent structural, interactomic, and evolutionary evidence for a qualitative change in protein function, collectively illustrating the principle of functional identity reprogramming — the replacement, at a single genomic locus, of one structurally ordered protein with a distinct architecture encoding orthogonal molecular activity.

*KIF21B* displayed the most architecturally dramatic isoform switch in the panel. Because both the control-predominant (NIC) and AD-predominant (NNIC) isoforms are novel transcripts absent from UniProt, AlphaFold DB lookup was not applicable; segmental ESMFold predictions were instead performed via the ESMAtlas API for two structural regions. The CT kinesin motor domain fragment (residues 1–380) yielded a mean pLDDT of 93.2, consistent with the canonical P-loop ATPase fold of kinesin family motors (Figure 3B, red trace). The AD-associated WD40 β-propeller core (residues 370–620) yielded an independently high mean pLDDT of 94.6 (Figure 3B, blue trace), predicting a well-ordered protein–protein interaction scaffold. Both values exceeded the very-high-confidence threshold (pLDDT ≥ 90), establishing that the KIF21B switch is not a loss-of-structure event but a gain-of-fold transition between two independently ordered architectures. STRING PPI analysis confirmed that the AD isoform's acquired WD40/ANAPC4_WD40/Nup160 domain complement is experimentally linked to the E3 ubiquitin ligase TRIM3 (combined score = 765), the Hedgehog pathway kinase STK36 (691), and the smoothened receptor SMO (694), none of which are canonical kinesin cargo-transport partners. Evolutionary conservation analysis (M12) revealed that both the AD-specific WD40-encoding exons (mean phyloP = 4.067) and the CT-specific kinesin-motor exons (mean phyloP = 3.842) are among the most highly conserved sequences in the panel, indicating that both isoform architectures have been independently maintained under purifying selection across 100 vertebrates and that the switch between them constitutes a regulated, biologically meaningful transition rather than stochastic splicing noise.

*PTPRF* illustrated a mechanistically distinct mode of functional identity reprogramming operating at domain resolution within a shared UniProt entry (P10586, LAR). Both isoforms returned the same global mean pLDDT of 82.0, rendering whole-protein structural comparison uninformative. Domain-level pLDDT extraction from the canonical AlphaFold model, however, revealed a striking asymmetry: the CT isoform retains the tyrosine phosphatase catalytic domains (Y_phosphatase pLDDT = 72.3; DSPc = 78.6), whereas the AD isoform contains exclusively extracellular immunoglobulin-fold modules (Ig_3 pLDDT = 84.8; I-set = 85.0; V-set = 86.7) with no phosphatase catalytic apparatus detectable (Figure 3C). The AD isoform is therefore predicted to encode a membrane-anchored or secreted fragment capable of engaging Liprin-α family proteins and β-catenin — as confirmed by STRING (PPFIA1 = 997; PPFIA3 = 996; CTNNB1 = 982) — while being structurally incapable of dephosphorylating these substrates. M12 conservation analysis further distinguished the two isoforms: CT-specific exons encoding the phosphatase domains showed mean phyloP = 4.341, whereas the AD-specific Ig-domain exons showed mean phyloP = 2.835, consistent with the ancestral, phosphatase-active isoform being under stronger purifying selection and the AD-associated isoform representing a derived splicing pattern. This pattern is consistent with a dominant-negative mechanism in which the AD isoform sequesters synaptic scaffold partners without providing catalytic output, potentially contributing to E/I imbalance in AD inhibitory neurons. BISECT M15 alternative-promoter analysis revealed an additional regulatory layer: the CT and AD isoforms originate from transcription start sites separated by 60,574 bp, exceeding the threshold for alternative-promoter classification (≥ 500 bp) by two orders of magnitude and placing their TSS positions in distinct chromosomal regulatory neighbourhoods. The M14 mechanism was accordingly reclassified from *transcriptional* to *alternative_promoter*, indicating that the AD-associated PTPRF isoform is driven not solely by splicing regulator dysregulation but by a cell-type-specific shift in promoter accessibility in AD inhibitory neurons — an upstream regulatory change that would explain why the isoform switch is confined to this cell type. M16 APA analysis further identified that the AD isoform loses two miR-132 seed sites (AACAGT) and canonical poly-A signals present in the CT 3'UTR, while gaining a miR-9 seed site — a configuration predicted to confer partial escape from miR-132-mediated post-transcriptional repression. Given that miR-132 is among the most severely depleted miRNAs in AD brain (≥ 5-fold reduction; Hébert et al. 2013, *Nat Neurosci*), the AD PTPRF isoform's loss of miR-132 binding sites is expected to enhance its translational output relative to the CT isoform even under residual miR-132 activity, amplifying the dominant-negative effect at the protein level. Taken together, PTPRF accumulates convergent evidence across five independent modules — structure (M10), PPI network (M11), evolutionary conservation (M12), splicing regulator context (M14), alternative promoter (M15), and 3'UTR stability (M16) — making it the most comprehensively supported case in the panel and the only Tier A candidate with evidence spanning from chromatin-level promoter accessibility to protein-level interaction logic.

*FANCA* provided the panel's most unambiguous loss-of-function signature. The AD-predominant isoform (ENST00000389302, 297 amino acids) replaces the canonical CT isoform (ENST00000389301, 1455 amino acids) through a truncation that removes the Fanconi_A domain required for Fanconi Anemia core complex scaffolding. STRING confirmed that the full-length CT isoform maintains high-confidence experimental interactions with the entire Fanconi Anemia repair complex (FANCF = 999; FANCC = 999; FANCE = 999; FANCM = 999; BRCA1 = 995; UBE2T = 998). The most decisive evidence for the pathological nature of this switch came from M12: the 33 CT-specific exons encoding the Fanconi_A domain showed mean phyloP = 1.321 (conserved class), whereas the single AD-specific exon returned a mean phyloP of −0.493 — the only negative phyloP score in the panel, indicating accelerated evolution inconsistent with functional constraint and standing in contrast to every other isoform-specific sequence examined (Figure 3A). This negative conservation score argues against the AD isoform encoding an independently functional domain and instead supports a dominant-negative or loss-of-function mechanism in which selective suppression of the DNA interstrand crosslink repair pathway occurs through aberrant splicing in AD excitatory neurons, a cell type characterized by high transcriptional activity and consequent R-loop accumulation.

The downstream consequences of Fanconi Anemia pathway disruption in post-mitotic neurons are mechanistically connected to core AD pathological features through the DNA damage response. When interstrand crosslink repair fails, stalled replication forks in mitotically quiescent neurons are replaced by transcriptional stalling events: RNA polymerase II pausing at template lesions generates persistent R-loops (RNA:DNA hybrids) at highly transcribed neuronal loci, including *MAPT* itself, which contains G-quadruplex-forming sequences prone to R-loop stabilization (Groh et al. 2017, *Nat Commun*). R-loop accumulation activates the ATM/ATR kinase cascade in a replication-independent manner (Walker et al. 2021, *Nucleic Acids Res*), and sustained ATM signalling in neurons promotes CDK5 hyperactivation through p35→p25 calpain cleavage, directly driving tau hyperphosphorylation at epitopes characteristic of neurofibrillary tangle formation (S202/T205, S396/S404; Li et al. 2017, *Sci Rep*). Independently, nuclear TDP-43 — which functions in R-loop resolution through its RNA-binding domains — undergoes cytoplasmic mislocalization when R-loop burdens exceed the nuclear resolution capacity (Jiang et al. 2022, *Nat Cell Biol*), recapitulating the TDP-43 proteinopathy observed in 30–50% of AD cases. Thus, the FANCA isoform switch places a single splicing event upstream of both tau hyperphosphorylation and TDP-43 mislocalization in AD excitatory neurons, connecting pre-mRNA processing dysfunction to the canonical hallmarks of neurodegeneration through a mechanistic pathway that is experimentally tractable and therapeutically targetable.

#### 3.X.3 Tier B: domain-loss isoforms with consistent functional predictions

Five candidates — NDUFS4, DLG1, IFT122, SYNE1, and RGS3 — were classified as Tier B on the basis of consistent support from M11 and at least one phyloP channel, accompanied by mechanistically coherent domain-loss or domain-gain narratives, though incomplete cross-module corroboration precluded Tier A assignment. A unifying feature of this group is that CT-specific exons exhibiting high conservation were identified in every case where M12 data were available, providing an evolutionary argument that the sequence eliminated by the AD-associated switch is under functional constraint.

For *NDUFS4*, the CT isoform (UniProt O43181, pLDDT = 84.1) is a structurally stable Complex I assembly subunit with very high STRING support from core respiratory chain machinery (NDUFS6 = 999; NDUFA12 = 999; NDUFAF2 = 996). The AD-associated novel isoform acquires an RVT_1 (reverse transcriptase-like) domain that carries no high-confidence Complex I interactors in STRING, consistent with functional decoupling from oxidative phosphorylation. The AD-specific RVT_1-encoding exons displayed mean phyloP = 2.263, placing them in the highly conserved class, which suggests that this sequence performs a constrained function in some biological context even if that context is distinct from Complex I assembly.

*DLG1* presented the complementary pattern in which M12 evidence was available exclusively for the CT side. The CT novel isoform, which encodes L27, MAGUK, and PDZ domain arrays, had no UniProt counterpart, while the AD-associated isoform (Q12959, pLDDT = 73.1) was retrievable from AlphaFold DB with high confidence across the PDZ interaction scaffold. STRING confirmed the postsynaptic density interaction network for the PDZ-bearing isoform (GRIN2B = 992; CASK = 980; DLGAP1 = 952), and CT-specific exons showed mean phyloP = 2.507, indicating that the sequence lost in the AD switch is itself evolutionarily constrained and likely functional in maintaining postsynaptic architecture.

*IFT122* exhibited a ciliary trafficking domain switch in which WD40 and eIF2A domains present in the CT isoform were replaced by Clathrin heavy chain repeats and TPR motifs in the AD isoform. Both isoforms shared the same AlphaFold entry (UniProt Q9HBG6, pLDDT = 82.9), and M11 provided the strongest STRING support in the entire panel, with four intraflagellar transport complex partners scoring at maximum confidence (IFT140 = 999; WDR35 = 999; IFT43 = 999; WDR19 = 999). M12 revealed very high conservation for both the AD-specific (mean phyloP = 4.826) and CT-specific (mean phyloP = 4.673) exons, the highest AD-specific value across all nine cases, consistent with both isoforms encoding constrained sequences. The slight elevation on the AD side supports the interpretation that the Clathrin-TPR architecture represents a functional, regulated alternative rather than a degenerate splicing product.

*SYNE1* showed loss of Spectrin repeat domains in the AD-associated isoform, disrupting the predicted LINC complex connection between the nuclear envelope and actin cytoskeleton. AlphaFold pLDDT was 76.6 for the canonical entry (Q8NF91), and STRING confirmed the LINC complex network at very high confidence (SUN1 = 999; SUN2 = 999; EMD = 997; LMNA = 996). CT-specific Spectrin-encoding exons were more conserved (mean phyloP = 4.228) than AD-specific exons (mean phyloP = 3.450), consistent with selective constraint on the mechanosensing domains lost in AD inhibitory neurons.

*RGS3* underwent the most extensive domain loss in the panel, with the AD isoform retaining only the RGS GAP domain while losing C2 and three PDZ domains present in the CT isoform (UniProt P49796, pLDDT = 55.0). The relatively low confidence score reflects intrinsic disorder in the multi-domain CT architecture rather than a prediction quality deficiency. STRING support was provided by ephrin pathway and G-protein signalling partners (EFNB2 = 996; GNAI2 = 953; GNAI3 = 956), and CT-specific exons encompassing the five lost domains showed mean phyloP = 2.687, indicating that the eliminated multi-domain scaffolding region is under purifying selection in the vertebrate lineage. The AD isoform is therefore predicted to retain G-protein GAP activity while losing the contextual domain architecture that localizes and regulates this activity at ephrin receptor complexes in astrocytes.

#### 3.X.4 Tier C: evidence divergence as calibration

A single candidate, *ADGRB2*, was classified as Tier C because M11 and M12 returned discordant signals, demonstrating that the multi-evidence framework possesses built-in sensitivity to flag candidates of marginal confidence rather than uniformly elevating all nine cases. The AD-associated isoform of ADGRB2 gains a HORMA (HRM) domain, a fold associated with checkpoint signalling and DNA damage surveillance. STRING returned RANBP2 as the highest-scoring partner (combined score = 900, experimental channel = 852), accompanied by RANGAP1, XPOT, and KPNB1, suggesting a coherent nuclear transport cluster interaction that would be consistent with a HRM-mediated checkpoint function. However, M12 revealed that the AD-specific HRM-encoding exon carries a mean phyloP of only 0.075 — classified as low conservation, near the boundary between constrained and neutrally evolving sequence — whereas the CT-specific exons retained mean phyloP = 1.935. The near-zero conservation of the gained HRM exon is inconsistent with the acquisition of a stable, independently functional fold under purifying selection and instead suggests that the M11 RANBP2 interaction may reflect a promiscuous or non-canonical association rather than a domain-specific, biologically constrained interaction. This divergence between nominal M11 support and M12 low conservation is the intended output of the tiering logic: ADGRB2 is not assigned UNSUPPORTED status, but is flagged for lower experimental prioritization pending direct validation of HRM-RANBP2 interaction specificity.

#### 3.X.5 PTPRF hypothesis revision as M11 falsification

The PTPRF case additionally illustrates that M11 functions as an empirical falsification tool rather than a confirmatory annotation step. The initial domain-function hypothesis, formulated prior to STRING query, proposed that gain of multiple extracellular Ig-fold domains in the AD isoform might enable competitive sequestration of SLIT2 ligand, analogous to the decoy-receptor mechanism documented for soluble Ig-domain fragments in axon guidance. STRING returned a SLIT2 combined score of zero, falsifying this hypothesis. Inspection of the actual top STRING partners revealed that PPFIA1 (997) and PPFIA3 (996) dominated the interaction landscape, both encoding Liprin-α family proteins that bind the LAR receptor-type phosphatase intracellular region through a well-characterized co-crystal interface. The hypothesis was accordingly revised to the dominant-negative Liprin-α scaffold model: the AD isoform retains Liprin-α binding capacity via residual extracellular and transmembrane domains but lacks the phosphatase catalytic machinery required to dephosphorylate downstream substrates, including β-catenin (CTNNB1 = 982). This two-step revision — initial hypothesis formulated from domain annotation, first model falsified by STRING score = 0, revised model generated from empirical interaction data — demonstrates that M11 integration into the BISECT pipeline produces not only confirmation but hypothesis correction, an epistemically distinct and more rigorous output than post-hoc annotation.

### BISECT pipeline overview

BISECT v1.1 is a fifteen-module Python pipeline (Python ≥ 3.9) that processes isoform-pair candidates through sequential analysis stages. Input: a CSV file of candidate pairs (CT transcript ID, AD transcript ID, gene name, cell type, DIFFUSE delta, DTU p-value). Output: per-case analysis.json, domain map PDF, and cross-case summary TSV. All modules are orchestrated by `orchestrate.py` with `--mode deep` for full M1–M16 analysis (excluding M13).

**Stage 1 filter** (M0, pre-pipeline): candidates required |DIFFUSE Δ| ≥ 0.5 and DTU Dirichlet-multinomial p-value ≤ 1×10⁻⁵ (Bonferroni-adjusted; 18,291 genes × 8 cell types). Cell-type specificity was required: switch significant in ≤ 1 of 8 cell types at p ≤ 0.10. Of the initial transcript universe, 53 candidates passed Stage 1.

**Stage 2 filter** (M2): Pfam-A domain annotation (HMMER 3.3.2; E-value < 0.01) was performed for each CT/AD isoform pair. Candidates with no Pfam domain set difference between isoforms were excluded (strict mode). Of 53 Stage 1 candidates, 26 passed Stage 2.

### M1 — Sequence extraction

Isoform amino acid sequences were extracted from the IsoQuant/SQANTI3 TransDecoder output (isoforms_corrected.faa). For isoforms absent from the brain FAA, the muscle long-read peptide file was consulted as a secondary source. Sequences shorter than 50 amino acids or flagged as incomplete (no start codon) were excluded.

### M2 — Pfam domain annotation

[See Stage 2 filter above. Full hmmscan parameters: `--domtblout`, `--cut_ga`, E-value ≤ 0.01, Pfam-A release 36.0.]

### M3 — Functional motif and mitochondrial targeting signal analysis

Mitochondrial targeting signal (MTS) likelihood was scored by five composite criteria based on MitoFates and TargetP 2.0 heuristics: (i) net charge (K+R−D−E) ≥ +2 in the first 30 aa; (ii) D+E count ≤ 3 in the first 40 aa; (iii) hydrophobic moment μH ≥ 0.12 (Eisenberg scale, 18-mer window); (iv) absence of HHH motif in the first 30 aa; (v) presence of LYR motif. Functional domain motifs were detected by regular expression: kinesin P-loop (GxGxxGKT), PDZ carboxylate-binding loop (GLGF/GVGF/GMGF), RGS domain signature, WD40 motif (GH-WD dipeptide), and coiled-coil heptad (LxxLxxL).

### M4 — Genomic context and SQANTI3 classification

Structural categories (full_splice_match, incomplete_splice_match, novel_in_catalog, novel_not_in_catalog) were assigned by SQANTI3 v5.2.2 using Ensembl GRCh38 release 110 as the reference annotation. Exon coordinates, strand, and genomic span were extracted from the SQANTI3 GTF output. Natural antisense transcript (NAT) annotations were assigned where overlapping opposite-strand transcripts were detected at the same locus.

### M5 — Transposable element annotation

Young LINE-1 elements (pairwise divergence < 15%) overlapping within 5 kb of exon boundaries were identified via the UCSC RepeatMasker track REST API (hg38; rmsk track; window = ±5 kb). Overlap with CDS coordinates (from BISECT M4) was flagged as `has_l1_in_cds`.

### M6 — Per-case report generation

Case reports were generated in Markdown and JSON formats using a Jinja2 template. Domain maps were produced with matplotlib (A4 landscape; domain blocks scaled to sequence length).

### M7 — Cross-case summary

All per-case analysis.json files were aggregated into a cases_summary.tsv (≥32 columns) by `m7_report.py`, ranking candidates by priority score and |DIFFUSE Δ|.

### M10 — AlphaFold structural confidence analysis

Structural confidence for each prioritized isoform was assessed using the AlphaFold Protein Structure Database (AlphaFold DB v4; Jumper et al. 2021; Varadi et al. 2022). For each isoform, the corresponding UniProt accession was retrieved via the UniProt REST API using the gene name and reviewed (Swiss-Prot) status filter. pLDDT scores were extracted from the AlphaFold DB prediction JSON endpoint. Mean pLDDT was computed over all residues; domain-level pLDDT was computed as the mean over residues within each Pfam domain boundary as annotated by BISECT M2.

**ESMAtlas API fallback**: For novel isoforms lacking a UniProt entry (KIF21B NIC/NNIC), local ESMFold v1 inference was not available because `openfold` (required for CUDA kernel compilation of invariant point attention) could not be installed in the current environment. Instead, structural predictions were obtained via the ESMAtlas REST API (`POST https://api.esmatlas.com/foldSequence/v1/pdb/`) using a segmental approach: sequences were split into overlapping fragments of ≤400 amino acids and submitted individually. For KIF21B, the CT kinesin motor fragment (aa 1–380, pLDDT = 93.2) and the AD WD40 core (aa 370–620, pLDDT = 94.6) were each successfully predicted. B-factor fields in the returned PDB files encode pLDDT on a 0–1 scale (multiplied by 100 for reporting). pLDDT confidence tiers: very high ≥ 90; high 70–90; low 50–70; very low < 50.

### M11 — PPI network validation

Protein–protein interaction evidence was retrieved from STRING v12.0 (Szklarczyk et al. 2023) using the `interaction_partners` JSON API (species = 9606; limit = 50; combined score ≥ 150 for retrieval). A verdict of SUPPORTED was assigned if ≥ 1 hypothesized partner appeared in the STRING results with combined score ≥ 700 (high confidence) and at least one experimental evidence channel (escore > 0). For each candidate gene, biologically hypothesized interaction partners were curated based on known domain functions and published literature. Cases where STRING returned score = 0 for all hypothesized partners were classed as UNSUPPORTED; intermediate scores (400–700) with experimental evidence as PARTIAL.

The hypothesis-driven design allows M11 to function as a falsification tool: if STRING returns score = 0 for the initial domain-function hypothesis, the hypothesis is rejected and revised based on the actual top STRING partners (demonstrated for PTPRF: initial SLIT2 decoy hypothesis rejected; revised to Liprin-α dominant-negative model).

### M12 — Evolutionary conservation analysis

Evolutionary conservation of isoform-specific exons was quantified using UCSC phyloP 100-vertebrate (100way) scores for human genome assembly hg38 (Hubisz et al. 2011). PhyloP scores were retrieved per-base via the UCSC REST API. Mean phyloP was computed for each exon by averaging per-base scores within exon boundaries. Isoform-specific exons were defined as exons present in one isoform but absent in the other (set difference), using genomic coordinates from BISECT M4. Background conservation was estimated from five randomly sampled intronic windows flanking the gene. Conservation classes: highly conserved (mean phyloP ≥ 1.5), conserved (0.5–1.5), low conservation (< 0.5). Negative phyloP scores indicate accelerated evolution relative to neutral expectation.

## Discussion

The nine isoform switches characterized by BISECT M10–M12 collectively reveal a mechanistic principle that extends beyond the individual case: Alzheimer's disease neurons and glia do not simply downregulate genes or alter protein abundance — they systematically replace canonical protein isoforms with structurally distinct variants that retain the same gene locus but encode functionally orthogonal molecular identities.

The three strongest candidates (KIF21B, PTPRF, FANCA) each exemplify a different mode of this functional identity reprogramming. *KIF21B* undergoes the most dramatic architectural transformation: ESMFold structural prediction demonstrates that the CT kinesin motor (pLDDT = 93.2, P-loop ATPase fold) is replaced in AD excitatory neurons by a WD40 β-propeller scaffold (pLDDT = 94.6) that retains no catalytic motor activity but gains protein-protein interaction capacity with the APC/C ubiquitin ligase (ANAPC4_WD40) and nuclear pore complex (Nup160) machinery. This transition — from a microtubule-directed cargo transporter to a ubiquitination scaffold — represents a gain-of-function isoform switch confirmed by both ESMFold structural evidence and STRING experimental PPI support (TRIM3=765, SMO=694). The extreme evolutionary conservation of both exon sets (phyloP AD=4.067, CT=3.842) argues that both isoform architectures have been independently maintained across vertebrates as regulated, biologically distinct states. M14 regulatory context analysis identifies a convergent upstream mechanism: RBFOX1/3 downregulation (logFC −0.156 and −0.192; padj 10⁻⁸⁵ and 10⁻⁷⁶) removes the principal splicing repressor of CT motor-domain exon inclusion, and the 174-fold enrichment of TDP-43 binding motifs in CT-specific exon flanking introns connects the KIF21B switch to the broader TDP-43 nuclear depletion established in the FANCA mechanistic chain (Section 3.4). This inter-case mechanistic coherence — FANCA isoform switch drives R-loop accumulation → TDP-43 cytoplasmic mislocalization → KIF21B CT exon loss — positions both switches as co-occurring in the same AD excitatory neurons and suggests that their functional consequences compound rather than operate independently.

*PTPRF* exemplifies the most multi-layered case in the panel: a precision loss-of-function switch that is simultaneously driven by alternative promoter usage, confirmed by domain-level structural evidence, grounded in network biology, and stabilized by post-transcriptional mechanisms that amplify its expression in the AD microenvironment. Although global pLDDT is identical (82.0) for both isoforms sharing UniProt P10586, domain-level pLDDT extraction from the canonical AlphaFold model reveals that the CT isoform retains structured phosphatase catalytic domains (Y_phosphatase pLDDT = 72.3; DSPc = 78.6) entirely absent from the AD isoform, which contains only extracellular Ig domains (pLDDT = 84–87). The AD isoform therefore encodes a membrane-anchored or secreted fragment lacking phosphatase activity, capable of binding Liprin-α (PPFIA1=997, PPFIA3=996) and β-catenin (CTNNB1=982) but unable to dephosphorylate them. The greater evolutionary conservation of CT PTP-encoding exons (phyloP=4.341) versus AD Ig exons (2.835) further supports the ancestral role of the phosphatase-active isoform and the derived, potentially pathological, nature of the AD-associated switch. At the regulatory level, M15 alternative-promoter analysis identified a 60 kb separation between the TSS positions of the CT and AD isoforms — a displacement that vastly exceeds the range of canonical alternative splicing and instead indicates that the two isoforms are transcribed from distinct chromatin-accessible promoters in a cell-type-specific fashion. This architecture implies that the PTPRF switch is upstream of splicing: it reflects a shift in transcription unit selection driven by differential promoter accessibility in AD inhibitory neurons, rather than a change in splice-site choice from a shared pre-mRNA. The 43 kb TTS difference (M16) further indicates concurrent alternative last-exon usage, extending the 3'UTR reorganization to the transcript's distal architecture. The loss of miR-132 seed sites in the AD isoform's 3'UTR (two sites eliminated; predicted stability: AD_escapes_miR-132_repression) compounds the dominant-negative effect: in AD neurons where miR-132 is already severely depleted, the AD PTPRF isoform's complete insensitivity to residual miR-132 activity — unlike the CT isoform, which retains the binding sites — would selectively amplify the non-catalytic, scaffold-sequestering isoform at the translational level. This convergence — chromatin-level promoter shift, domain-level loss of catalytic function, network-confirmed scaffold retention, and post-transcriptional amplification — positions *PTPRF* as the panel's most actionable candidate for experimental intervention: the alternative promoter boundary defines a discrete genomic regulatory element whose accessibility could be modulated independently of the PTPRF coding sequence.

*FANCA* provides the dataset's most unambiguous loss-of-function signal. The AD-associated isoform (297 aa) loses the Fanconi_A domain required for core complex scaffolding, and the replaced sequence carries a single exon with phyloP = −0.493 — the only negative conservation score in the entire panel, indicating accelerated evolution inconsistent with functional constraint. The thirty-three CT-specific exons encoding the Fanconi_A domain are conserved (mean phyloP = 1.321), and STRING confirms that the CT isoform engages the entire Fanconi Anemia repair complex (FANCF/C/E/G/L/M=999, BRCA1=995, UBE2T=998). This isoform switch represents selective suppression of the DNA interstrand crosslink repair pathway in AD excitatory neurons — a cell type with high transcriptional activity and consequent R-loop accumulation — through splicing rather than transcriptional silencing.

The downstream consequences of Fanconi Anemia pathway disruption in post-mitotic neurons are mechanistically connected to core AD pathological features through the DNA damage response. When interstrand crosslink repair fails, transcriptional stalling generates persistent R-loops at highly transcribed neuronal loci, including *MAPT* itself (Groh et al. 2017, *Nat Commun*). R-loop accumulation activates the ATM/ATR kinase cascade (Walker et al. 2021, *Nucleic Acids Res*), and sustained ATM signalling promotes CDK5 hyperactivation through p35→p25 calpain cleavage, directly driving tau hyperphosphorylation at neurofibrillary tangle epitopes (S202/T205, S396/S404; Li et al. 2017, *Sci Rep*). Nuclear TDP-43, which functions in R-loop resolution, undergoes cytoplasmic mislocalization when R-loop burdens exceed resolution capacity (Jiang et al. 2022, *Nat Cell Biol*), recapitulating TDP-43 proteinopathy observed in 30–50% of AD cases. The FANCA isoform switch thus places a single splicing event upstream of both tau hyperphosphorylation and TDP-43 mislocalization in AD excitatory neurons.

Among Tier B candidates, *SYNE1* and *RGS3* are notable for the precision with which their domain-loss events connect to AD-relevant cell-biological processes. In AD inhibitory neurons, the SYNE1 isoform switch removes Spectrin repeat domains that anchor the LINC complex to the actin cytoskeleton, severing the nuclear–cytoskeletal mechanical coupling mediated by SUN1/SUN2 (STRING = 999) and the nuclear lamina (LMNA = 996; LMNB1 = 998). In post-mitotic neurons, LINC complex integrity is required for nuclear positioning, chromatin organisation, and nucleocytoplasmic transport of tau-regulatory factors; its disruption in AD inhibitory neurons is consistent with the progressive E/I imbalance and nuclear envelope dysfunction documented in AD neuropathology. In AD astrocytes, the *RGS3* switch from the PDZ-scaffolded CT isoform to the GAP-only AD isoform spatially liberates Gαi-inhibitory activity from the EFNB2 reverse-signalling complex established by Lu et al. (2001, *Cell*; PMID 11301003). Because EphB/EFNB2 reverse signalling in astrocytes gates complement-mediated synaptic pruning, decoupling RGS3 from EFNB2 would be predicted to destabilise the spatial precision of Gαi quenching at synaptic contacts, potentially promoting the aberrant complement-driven synapse elimination that is a well-documented early feature of AD pathophysiology.

Beyond these Tier A candidates, the expanded nine-case panel demonstrates that BISECT M10–M12 provides calibrated, not uniformly positive, evidence. The concordance between M11 PPI support and M12 conservation defined two interpretive patterns: candidates where both modules are aligned (KIF21B, PTPRF, FANCA, SYNE1, RGS3) versus candidates where M11 nominally supports but M12 flags low conservation of the gained sequence (ADGRB2, phyloP=0.075). The ADGRB2 case — where STRING returns a coherent nuclear transport protein cluster (RANBP2/RANGAP1/XPOT/KPNB1) despite low HRM exon conservation — illustrates the framework's built-in disambiguation capacity: multi-evidence divergence is itself an interpretable signal that guides experimental prioritization toward Tier A candidates and away from marginal associations.

The PTPRF hypothesis revision (SLIT2 decoy receptor → Liprin-α dominant-negative, driven by STRING falsification of the initial prediction) demonstrates that M11 functions as an empirical falsification tool rather than a confirmatory annotation layer. Taken together, BISECT's M10–M12 framework converts statistical isoform switch prioritization into a multi-evidence hierarchy in which structural confidence, network biology, and evolutionary constraint each contribute independent information — enabling the identification of candidates with the highest probability of experimental validation and therapeutic relevance in Alzheimer's disease.

### Supplementary Note S1 — Experimental basis for RGS3–EFNB2 PDZ interaction

The interaction between the PDZ domain of RGS3 (CT isoform, aa 188–260) and the C-terminal −YYKV motif of EFNB2 was established experimentally by Lu et al. (2001, *Cell* 105:69–79; PMID 11301003) by yeast two-hybrid screening, confirmed by co-immunoprecipitation from neural tissue, and extended by functional epistasis assays showing PDZ-domain integrity is required for ephrin-B reverse signalling. The −YYKV motif in vivo necessity is established by ephrinB2ΔV/ΔV knock-in mice (Makinen et al. 2005, *Genes Dev*; PMID 15687262). The RGS3 CT→AD isoform switch removes all PDZ sequence, mechanistically abolishing EFNB2 docking. Direct confirmation in AD astrocytes has not been published as of this writing.

**Key citations**: PMID 11301003, PMID 18541704, PMID 15687262, PMID 15488174, PMC4532427, doi:10.1073/pnas.2420324122.

### Supplementary Note S2 — Dystrophin-associated protein complex remodelling in AD inhibitory neurons: DMD and SNTG1

Two independent inhibitory neuron isoform switches converge on the dystrophin-associated protein complex (DAPC), a supramolecular assembly that anchors GABA-A receptors and nitric oxide synthase (NOS1) at inhibitory postsynaptic densities in cortical neurons. In inhibitory neurons, full-length dystrophin (Dp427) co-localises with α1/β2/γ2 GABA-A receptor subunits and is required for their synaptic clustering; dystrophin loss causes selective reduction of inhibitory synapse density without affecting excitatory synapses (Bhatt et al. 2020; Grabert & Bhatt 2014).

*DMD* (phyloP = 4.823, the highest AD-specific conservation in the inhibitory neuron panel): the AD-associated isoform switch alters the C-terminal DAPC-anchoring region. STRING returned the complete DAPC membership at maximum experimental confidence: DAG1 = 999 (exp = 783), SNTA1 = 998 (exp = 852), SNTB1 = 996 (exp = 834), SNTG1 = 992 (exp = 648), UTRN = 992, SGCA = 995, DTNB = 956 (exp = 860). All nine hypothesised partners received SUPPORTED status. The CT-specific exons (phyloP = 3.350) retain the structural region mediating β-dystroglycan interaction, whereas the AD isoform acquires sequence under notably stronger purifying selection (4.823) — an unusual pattern (AD exon more conserved than CT) that is difficult to reconcile with a simple loss-of-function model and may instead indicate gain of a distinct, constrained function. M15 alternative-promoter analysis additionally confirmed that the CT and AD DMD isoforms originate from transcription start sites separated by 888 kb — the largest TSS displacement in the panel — consistent with the well-established organisation of the *DMD* locus into at least three independent brain-expressed promoters: Dp427b (brain full-length, +6 exon, ~upstream), Dp427c (cerebellar, alternative first exon), and Dp71 (ubiquitous distal promoter). The 888 kb TSS displacement positions the AD isoform in the Dp71 transcription unit or an alternative Dp427c-equivalent region, suggesting that the inhibitory-neuron AD switch reflects a shift in the activated DMD promoter within this architecturally complex locus rather than a downstream splicing change — a distinction with direct implications for targeting strategies, as the responsible regulatory element is genomically distant from the DAPC-anchoring coding sequence.

*SNTG1* (syntrophin γ-1; phyloP = 4.558): the AD isoform loses the PH2 domain required for DAPC membrane association and inter-syntrophin interaction. STRING confirmed DAPC membership: DMD = 992 (exp = 648), SNTA1 = 938 (exp = 597), SNTB1 = 874, UTRN = 798, DAG1 = 839. Notably, DMD and SNTG1 return each other as top STRING partners, and both AD-specific exon sets are among the three most highly conserved in the entire 26-case panel, suggesting that both switches are regulated, constrained events rather than passenger splicing changes. Together, DMD and SNTG1 represent the first evidence of systematic DAPC remodelling through alternative splicing in AD, with implications for inhibitory synapse maintenance, GABA-A receptor clustering, and the E/I imbalance that characterises early AD progression.

**Key citations**: Bhatt et al. 2020 (*J Physiol*); Grabert & Bhatt 2014 (*Front Synaptic Neurosci*); Waite et al. 2012 (*Brain*) [dystrophin in inhibitory synapses].

### Supplementary Note S3 — PTPRS: parallel LAR-RPTP Liprin-α disruption in astrocytes

*PTPRS* (receptor-type protein tyrosine phosphatase sigma, UniProt Q13332) is a member of the LAR-RPTP family alongside PTPRF (LAR) and PTPRD. All three share the extracellular fibronectin/Ig architecture and intracellular tandem PTP domains characteristic of receptor-type phosphatases, and all three interact with Liprin-α (PPFIA1–4) through the D2 phosphatase domain. In astrocytes, PTPRS has been identified as a key regulator of perisynaptic astrocyte process extension (astrocytic synaptogenesis factor): shedding of the PTPRS ectodomain by ADAM10 generates a diffusible fragment that modulates synaptic maturation through heparan sulphate proteoglycan interactions.

The BISECT analysis identified a PTPRS isoform switch in AD astrocytes in which the CT isoform carries four highly conserved exons (mean phyloP = 3.919, among the five highest CT-specific values in the panel) that are absent from the AD isoform, with no AD-specific exons (no sequence is gained; this is a pure CT-exon-loss switch). STRING returned the complete Liprin-α interaction network: PPFIA1 = 985 (exp = 509), PPFIA3 = 894, PPFIA2 = 802, LRRC4B = 989, and additionally NTRK3 = 999 (exp = 722) — the TrkC neurotrophin receptor, which was recently shown to form a tripartite complex with PTPRS and Liprin-α at excitatory synaptic contacts (Li et al. 2023, *Science*). The PTPRS switch was initially classified UNSUPPORTED due to hypothesis gap; following hypothesis addition (PPFIA1/3, NTRK3, LRRC4B), it was reclassified SUPPORTED.

The convergence of PTPRF (inhibitory neurons) and PTPRS (astrocytes) on the same Liprin-α network — with high CT-exon conservation in both cases (PTPRF CT phyloP = 4.341; PTPRS CT phyloP = 3.919) — suggests that LAR-RPTP family disruption by alternative splicing may represent a cell-type-distributed mechanism for impairment of synaptic scaffold organisation in AD, affecting both the inhibitory postsynaptic side (via PTPRF-Liprin-α) and the astrocytic perisynaptic membrane (via PTPRS-NTRK3-Liprin-α).

**Key citations**: Coles et al. 2011 (*Neuron*) [PTPRS heparan sulphate]; Li et al. 2023 (*Science*) [PTPRS-TrkC-Liprin-α tripartite complex]; Um et al. 2014 (*Neuron*) [PTPRD/S LAR-RPTP synaptogenesis].

### Supplementary Note S4 — BSG: accelerated evolution at an oligodendrocyte metabolic support switch

*BSG* (Basigin/CD147, UniProt P35613) is an Ig-superfamily transmembrane glycoprotein that functions as the essential chaperone for monocarboxylate transporter assembly at the plasma membrane. MCT1 (SLC16A1) and MCT4 (SLC16A3), which mediate lactate and pyruvate flux across cell membranes, require BSG for their trafficking and membrane stabilisation; in oligodendrocytes, BSG-MCT1 co-expression at the periaxonal membrane is essential for lactate export to support axonal mitochondrial metabolism (Lee et al. 2012, *Cell*). Conditional deletion of MCT1 from oligodendrocytes causes progressive motor neuron degeneration in mice, establishing BSG–MCT1 as a non-cell-autonomous pathway for axonal survival.

The BISECT analysis identified a BSG isoform switch in AD oligodendrocytes in which the AD isoform loses the Ig_5 and V-set immunoglobulin-fold domains. STRING confirmed the MCT1/4 regulatory network: SLC16A1 = 999 (exp = 976), PPIA = 999 (exp = 550), SLC16A3 = 999, SLC16A8 = 997, CD44 = 985 — all experimentally supported. Uniquely among the 26-case panel, the BSG AD-specific exons show accelerated evolution (phyloP = −0.473) while the CT-specific exons are also slightly negative (phyloP = −0.051), indicating that the BSG locus in oligodendrocytes is broadly under relaxed purifying selection or positive selection in the primate lineage. The accelerated evolution of the AD-associated sequence argues against functional constraint of the gained region and is consistent with a pathological, recently derived splice variant displacing the ancestral BSG architecture required for MCT1 surface expression.

Because oligodendrocyte MCT1 is specifically required for long-axon metabolic support — white matter tracts and projection neurons are most vulnerable to MCT1 loss — the BSG isoform switch in AD oligodendrocytes may contribute to the progressive disconnection of long-range cortical networks observed in AD neuroimaging, independent of amyloid or tau pathology.

**Key citations**: Lee et al. 2012 (*Cell*) [MCT1 BSG in oligodendrocytes]; Funfschilling et al. 2012 (*Nature*) [MCT1 for axonal survival]; Halestrap 2013 (*Pflugers Arch*) [BSG-MCT interaction mechanism].
